================================================================================
TWO-STAGE TEMPERATURE APPROACH
================================================================================

OPTION 1: Start High, Then Cool Down
• Stage 1 (0-12 hours): 104-110°F
  - Benefits: Optimal for S. thermophilus, L. delbrueckii, L. helveticus, L. bulgaricus
  - These thermophiles establish quickly and create yogurt texture
• Stage 2 (12-36 hours): 98°F
  - Benefits: Optimal for most probiotics, saves L. buchneri, L. curvatus, L. sakei
  - Allows mesophiles to multiply

Pros:
+ Maximizes thermophile growth (yogurt texture)
+ Saves 3 probiotics that die at 99°F
+ Still optimal for most probiotics in stage 2
+ May produce better overall CFU counts

Cons:
- More complex (requires temperature change)
- L. kefiri still dies (max 95°F)
- Leuconostoc mesenteroides still dies (max 86°F)
- Some probiotics may be stressed by temperature change

OPTION 2: Start Low, Then Warm Up
• Stage 1 (0-12 hours): 95°F
  - Benefits: Saves L. kefiri, optimal for B. subtilis
  - Allows lower-temperature probiotics to establish
• Stage 2 (12-36 hours): 99°F
  - Benefits: Optimal for most probiotics
  - Allows thermophiles to work

Pros:
+ Saves L. kefiri (in addition to the 3 at 98°F)
+ Optimal for B. subtilis in stage 1
+ Still optimal for most probiotics in stage 2

Cons:
- More complex (requires temperature change)
- L. buchneri, L. curvatus, L. sakei die in stage 2 (99°F)
- Leuconostoc mesenteroides still dies (max 86°F)
- Thermophiles (S. thermophilus, etc.) are suboptimal in stage 1

RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================

RECOMMENDATION 1: Stay at 99°F (SIMPLEST)
• Pros: Simple, optimal for 28 probiotics, proven to work
• Cons: 5 probiotics die (but they're not core therapeutic strains)
• Best for: If your current yogurt is working well and you're seeing benefits

RECOMMENDATION 2: Lower to 98°F (BALANCED)
• Pros: Saves 3 probiotics (L. buchneri, L. curvatus, L. sakei), still optimal for 28 probiotics
• Cons: Slightly worse for thermophiles (but still survivable), L. kefiri and Leuconostoc still die
• Best for: If you want to maximize probiotic diversity without complexity

RECOMMENDATION 3: Two-Stage (104°F → 98°F) (MOST COMPLEX, POTENTIALLY BEST)
• Pros: Maximizes thermophile growth, saves 3 probiotics, optimal for most in stage 2
• Cons: Requires temperature change, more complex setup
• Best for: If you want maximum CFU counts and don't mind the complexity

RECOMMENDATION 4: Lower to 95°F (NOT RECOMMENDED)
• Pros: Saves 4 probiotics (including L. kefiri)
• Cons: Suboptimal for 28 probiotics, at minimum survival for 2 probiotics
• Best for: NOT RECOMMENDED - too low for most probiotics

IMPORTANT CONSIDERATIONS
================================================================================

1. CORE THERAPEUTIC STRAINS:
   • L. reuteri (DSM 17938 & ATCC PTA 6475) - optimal at 96-100°F
   • L. gasseri (BNR17) - optimal at 98-100°F
   • B. subtilis (HU58) - optimal at 90-95°F
   All three are optimal or survivable at 98-99°F

2. YOGURT TEXTURE:
   • S. thermophilus, L. delbrueckii, L. helveticus, L. bulgaricus create texture
   • These prefer 104-113°F but are survivable at 98-99°F
   • At 98°F, texture may be slightly less thick, but still good

3. THE 5 THAT DIE AT 99°F:
   • L. buchneri, L. curvatus, L. sakei - food preservation/fermentation specialists
   • L. kefiri - kefir-specific probiotic
   • Leuconostoc mesenteroides - fermentation starter
   • These are NOT your core therapeutic strains for SIBO
   • They're nice-to-have but not essential

4. 36-HOUR FERMENTATION:
   • Long fermentation allows probiotics to multiply significantly
   • Even suboptimal temperatures can produce high CFU counts over 36 hours
   • The most important thing is that core therapeutic strains are optimal
